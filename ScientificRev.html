The Scientific Revolution

The Scientific Revolution was a time period (approx. 1500 - 1700) during the Renaissance where Europeans shifted away from religious thinking and instead focused more on scientific reasoning.

An example of mathematical patterns found in Leonardo Da Vinci's paintings. The golden ratio was first discovered by Euclid, a Greek mathematician. Paintings started to use it, showing mathematical thinking of things during this time period. This is an example of a person (Da Vinci) and ideas (mathematical thinking). Leonardo Da Vinci was trying to make scientific concepts for flight, including ornithopters, "helicopters", etc. which all sadly didn't really work, (probably except for the parachute) but it paved the way for scientific reasoning later on for people like the Wright Brothers who used airfoils that were a concept found in a lot of flying concepts Da Vinci thought of. Da Vinci was a key figure during the Renaissance, helping with a lot of scientific concepts not limited to the above.


More Renaissance: the Plague, Printing Press, etc.

The Renaissance is a really big impactful event for influencing the Scientific Revolution. People like Da Vinci (Above) helped pave the way for future scientific innovations. The "Black Death" or plague started to make many turn to secularism or skepticism, questioning why god would let such a horrible event occur and let many die. This started to make people focus more on medicine and the human body, in order to cure diseaases. The Renaissance really started to make people start to turn away from religious beliefs and think more about using evidence, reasoning, and scientific knowledge to understand the world around us. With the more efficient printing press invented by Johann Gutenberg, many ideas and knowledge started to spread faster, not limited to science. People like Rene Descartes (France) and Francis Bacon (England) created the scientific method which would then be used in further scientific development.

Some Key Scientific People Who Influenced the Scientific Revolution

Isaac Newton was an English mathematician, physicist, astronomer, one of the fathers of calculus, etc. who made the laws of motion, discovered gravity, created reflecting telscopes, and much more. Hes probably the most famous for his universal law of gravitation, which states that everything in the universe attracts each other with a force directly proportional to the product of the objects' masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their COM. Basically, gravity is strongest when 2 objects have a lot mass and the distance between them is small. If they are further apart or lighter, gravity is less strong.

Nicolaus Copernicus was a Polish astronomer and mathematician who proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system, which used the sun as the center of our solar system instead of the earth, which was previously used. In addition to having a major contribution to astronomy, he also studied medicine. During the time period, many actually thought that astronomy and the position of planets in orbit impacted humans and illnesses.

Galileo Galilei was an Italian astronomer, physicist, mathematician, and philosopher who made the best refracting telescope at the time to prove that Nicolaus Copernicus was right about the heliocentric theory. He observed the moon's craters & mountains, the sun's dark spots, Jupiter's moons, the rings of saturn, how the milky way galaxy consisted of a ton of stars, and more. He also studied motion and kinematics, to try to understand more about how forces acted on things (in earth) by experimenting with inclined planes. He discovered Inertia, and later on Isaac Newton created the law of inertia (Newton's 1st law).

Johannes Kepler was a German astronomer, mathematician, and writer who confirmed Copernicus' heliocentric theory using math and his own laws of planetary motion unlike Galileo who actually saw it using a telescope. Kepler explained the movement of planets around the sun through his laws of planetary motion. He described how (1) planet orbits aren't perfect circles, they are more of an ellipse, and that the sun is at one focus. So, the distance of the planet to the sun varies significantly unless the orbit is more circular, or the foci are closer to the center. (2) that a planet sweeps the same amount of area (portion of the ellipse area by a line connecting the earth to the sun) in the same amount of time (3) that the square of a planet's orbital period (the time it takes to make 1 revolution) is proportional to the cube of half the length of the orbit's major axis (longer "diameter") of the ellipse.
